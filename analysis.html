2.1.3 Interpretation

A simple linear regression was used to estimate the association between total expenditures per pupil and graduation rates across Massachusetts school districts. The results indicate that every $10,000 increase in per-pupil spending is associated with an increase of approximately 2.3 percentage points in the graduation rate. This effect is statistically significant at conventional levels (p < 0.001), suggesting that the relationship is unlikely to be the result of random variation.

However, the model’s explanatory power is limited. The R-squared value is 0.013, indicating that only 1.3 percent of the variation in graduation rates is explained by differences in per-pupil spending. This suggests that other factors—such as socioeconomic conditions, instructional quality, or school leadership—play a more substantial role in influencing outcomes.

While the model reveals a statistically detectable relationship between spending and graduation rates, it remains descriptive. No causal inference can be drawn from these results. The possibility of reverse causality or omitted variable bias must be considered, particularly given that higher-performing districts may receive more funding or that both funding and graduation rates may be influenced by a third variable such as community wealth or political support for education.

2.1.5 Interpretation

Separate linear regression models were estimated for school districts grouped by the majority racial composition of their student population. This approach helps assess whether the strength of the spending–graduation relationship differs across demographic contexts.

In Majority Hispanic districts, the slope coefficient is approximately 0.00135, indicating that a $10,000 increase in per-pupil spending is associated with a 13.5 percentage point increase in graduation rate. The t-statistic is 5.33, and the relationship is statistically significant at the 1 percent level, despite a smaller sample size.

In Majority Black districts, the estimated slope is 0.00062, suggesting a 6.2 percentage point increase in graduation rate per $10,000 spent. This relationship is also statistically significant (t = 2.62, p ≈ 0.012), but the relatively small number of observations (n = 41) reduces precision.

In Majority White districts, which comprise the majority of the sample (n = 3,419), the slope is 0.00029, corresponding to a 2.9 percentage point increase in graduation rate per $10,000 in spending. The t-statistic of 9.94 and very low p-value reflect the stability and statistical strength of this estimate.

The t-statistics measure how far each coefficient lies from zero in terms of standard error units, and they confirm that the positive associations observed are unlikely to occur by chance. Although the strength of the effect varies by group, the direction is consistent, suggesting that increased education funding tends to support higher graduation outcomes across different demographic contexts. Larger effects in Majority Hispanic and Majority Black districts may reflect greater marginal benefits from additional investment in historically underserved areas.
